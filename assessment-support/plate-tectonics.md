# Chapter 1 Assessment Guide: Plate Tectonics

## Source and Format

This guide supports introductory undergraduate assessment design from the revision-bound Wikipedia topic corpus recorded for this package. It is design guidance rather than a finished set of scored materials. Parameter ranges are chosen to keep introductory calculations interpretable; course authors should check any locally substituted values.

## General Assessment Priorities

- Ask learners to reason from relative motion rather than recall isolated landforms.
- Separate evidence, inference, and model limitation in explanations.
- Use diagrams, maps, and small datasets as well as prose.
- Make “half-spreading” versus “full opening” explicit in every rate task.
- Reward qualified claims when a real boundary or basin departs from an idealized model.

## Objective 1.1a: Distinguish compositional layers from mechanical layers and explain why the lithosphere can move as plates.

### Target understanding

Mastery means that a learner can distinguish crust from lithosphere, place the lithospheric mantle correctly, and explain that the asthenosphere is mostly solid yet capable of long-term viscous deformation. The learner should also recognize that a plate may carry both oceanic and continental crust.

### Question guides

#### Guide A — Classification from evidence

- **Variables and ranges:** boundary depth (20–250 km); evidence type (chemical composition, seismic velocity, elastic strength, long-term deformation); setting (ridge, craton, ocean basin).
- **Constraint or relationship:** give one or more observations and ask whether they define a compositional or mechanical boundary; solve for the justified label, not a numerical depth.
- **Contexts:** interpretation of a seismic profile; drilling record; comparison of a craton and ridge.
- **Answer formats:** multiple choice with category-confusion distractors; short answer naming evidence; worked explanation with a labeled layer sequence.
- **Worked instantiation:** A boundary at 110 km separates rigid rock above from material that deforms over long times. Answer: it supports the lithosphere–asthenosphere boundary because the evidence is mechanical.

#### Guide B — Compare two columns

- **Variables and ranges:** oceanic age (0–170 Ma); setting (young ridge, old ocean basin, continental craton); qualitative thickness and density.
- **Constraint or relationship:** learners rank lithospheric thickness or density using cooling history while holding the distinction between crust and lithosphere.
- **Contexts:** two ocean-floor locations; an oceanic location versus a stable continent; a geophysical survey plan.
- **Answer formats:** ranking; short justification; annotated cross-section.
- **Worked instantiation:** Rank oceanic lithosphere at 5 Ma and 120 Ma by thickness. Answer: the 120 Ma lithosphere is thicker because conductive cooling converts more upper mantle into the mechanical lithosphere.

#### Guide C — Analogy critique

- **Variables and ranges:** analogy type (liquid ocean, conveyor belt, wax, putty); timescale (seconds to millions of years); stress duration.
- **Constraint or relationship:** ask what the analogy captures and where it fails; the correct response must state that the asthenosphere is not globally molten.
- **Contexts:** museum caption; student explanation; popular-science animation.
- **Answer formats:** multiple choice; two-sentence critique; revision of an inaccurate caption.
- **Worked instantiation:** “Plates float on an underground magma sea.” Answer: reject; plates overlie mostly solid asthenosphere that deforms viscously on geologic timescales.

#### Guide D — Error analysis

- **Variables and ranges:** one planted misconception per prompt: crust = lithosphere, asthenosphere = liquid, continents = whole plates, Moho = mechanical boundary.
- **Constraint or relationship:** identify the single error and replace it with a precise statement.
- **Contexts:** exam response; textbook sketch; seismic-lab summary.
- **Answer formats:** short answer; corrected diagram label; worked correction.
- **Worked instantiation:** “A plate ends at the continental coastline.” Answer: a plate can include continental land and adjacent oceanic lithosphere; its boundary is determined by relative motion and deformation.

### Design guidance by purpose

| Purpose | Cognitive level | How to design the task |
|---|---|---|
| Homework | Apply/analyze | Compare layer classifications across two settings and require an annotated cross-section. |
| Group discussion | Evaluate | Critique competing analogies for solid-state flow. |
| Quiz | Understand/apply | Use one evidence statement and ask for the justified boundary type. |
| Exam | Analyze | Combine seismic, temperature, and behavior clues; score evidence-based distinctions. |
| Project/activity | Create | Build a physical or digital model that shows both classification schemes without conflation. |

## Objective 1.2a: Classify plate boundaries from relative motion and predict characteristic geological expressions.

### Target understanding

Mastery means using relative velocity first, then predicting crustal budget, landforms, volcanism, and earthquake depth. Learners should distinguish subduction from continental collision and recognize oblique or broad boundary zones.

### Question guides

#### Guide A — Motion-vector classification

- **Variables and ranges:** perpendicular velocity from -100 to +100 mm/yr; parallel velocity from -100 to +100 mm/yr; sign convention supplied; plate pair orientation.
- **Constraint or relationship:** classify by the dominant perpendicular component and identify obliquity when the parallel component is substantial.
- **Contexts:** GPS stations; offset ridge segment; continental fault zone; trench-parallel motion.
- **Answer formats:** multiple choice; vector sketch; worked component interpretation.
- **Worked instantiation:** 30 mm/yr convergence plus 18 mm/yr parallel motion gives an obliquely convergent boundary with likely partitioned shear.

#### Guide B — Observation-to-boundary inference

- **Variables and ranges:** earthquake depths (0–700 km), topographic feature (ridge, trench, mountain belt), volcanic pattern (none, line, arc), crust types.
- **Constraint or relationship:** provide three observations; ask for the best boundary interpretation and one observation that would test it.
- **Contexts:** island arc; ocean ridge; continental collision; transform system.
- **Answer formats:** evidence table; short claim-evidence-reasoning response; oral explanation.
- **Worked instantiation:** A trench, arc volcanism, and earthquakes deepening landward support oceanic subduction beneath an overriding plate.

#### Guide C — Compare convergent settings

- **Variables and ranges:** pairs of crust type (oceanic/oceanic, oceanic/continental, continental/continental); relative density; age of oceanic lithosphere (10–170 Ma).
- **Constraint or relationship:** predict which material descends and which surface features form; continental collision should emphasize thickening, not deep sustained subduction of both continents.
- **Contexts:** Andes-type margin; island arc; Himalayan-type collision.
- **Answer formats:** matching; comparative paragraph; labeled cross-sections.
- **Worked instantiation:** Old oceanic lithosphere approaching a continent is expected to descend, forming a trench, dipping seismic zone, and continental volcanic arc.

#### Guide D — Boundary-map error analysis

- **Variables and ranges:** one incorrect symbol or prediction; map scale 100–2,000 km; boundary geometry straight, curved, or segmented.
- **Constraint or relationship:** locate the inconsistency between arrows and predicted geology.
- **Contexts:** hazard map; student-drawn plate map; ridge-transform network.
- **Answer formats:** mark and correct; short explanation; revised legend.
- **Worked instantiation:** Arrows diverge across a fault but the key predicts a deep earthquake zone. Correction: divergence mainly produces shallow earthquakes; deepening seismicity indicates a descending slab.

### Design guidance by purpose

| Purpose | Cognitive level | How to design the task |
|---|---|---|
| Homework | Apply | Rotate boundary orientation and require vector-based classification before feature prediction. |
| Group discussion | Analyze | Give incomplete evidence and ask groups what additional data would discriminate models. |
| Quiz | Apply | Present arrows and ask for boundary class plus one expected feature. |
| Exam | Analyze/evaluate | Use an oblique boundary with mixed observations and score qualified reasoning. |
| Project/activity | Create | Interpret a regional boundary using public earthquake and topographic data. |

## Objective 1.3a: Trace the creation, cooling, thickening, and recycling of oceanic lithosphere and compare contributions to plate motion.

### Target understanding

Mastery means connecting ridge volcanism to crust formation, age to cooling and lithospheric thickening, cooling to density and subsidence, and old oceanic lithosphere to subduction. Learners should describe motion as a coupled force balance rather than a universal magma-push or simple conveyor mechanism.

### Question guides

#### Guide A — Sequence and causal chain

- **Variables and ranges:** four to seven process cards; ages 0–170 Ma; ridge-to-trench distance 500–8,000 km.
- **Constraint or relationship:** order creation, outward motion, cooling, thickening, density increase, and subduction; require two causal links.
- **Contexts:** parcel of basaltic crust; ocean-basin transect; ship-based heat-flow survey.
- **Answer formats:** ordered list; flowchart in words; short explanation.
- **Worked instantiation:** Correct order: ridge formation → outward motion → conductive cooling → thicker/denser lithosphere → possible subduction.

#### Guide B — Crustal-area calculation

- **Variables and ranges:** ridge length 500–10,000 km; full spreading rate 10–200 mm/yr; duration 10,000–10,000,000 yr.
- **Constraint or relationship:** area = ridge length × full widening; convert mm/yr to km/yr; state constant-rate assumption.
- **Contexts:** global ridge segment; opening young sea; comparing slow and fast ridges.
- **Answer formats:** short numerical answer with units; multiple choice with conversion distractors; full worked solution.
- **Worked instantiation:** A 1,500-km segment at 60 mm/yr creates 0.090 km²/yr of new seafloor area.

#### Guide C — Driving-force comparison

- **Variables and ranges:** setting with or without a long subducting slab; ridge elevation; plate speed 0–160 mm/yr; qualitative mantle-flow information.
- **Constraint or relationship:** ask which contribution is plausible and what evidence would be needed; no setting is solved by naming one universal force.
- **Contexts:** Pacific-type plate; Atlantic ridge plate with little subduction; numerical geodynamic-model summary.
- **Answer formats:** ranking with confidence; short evidence statement; comparison matrix.
- **Worked instantiation:** A plate attached to a cold, long sinking slab plausibly experiences strong slab pull, but the full motion still depends on boundary resistance and mantle coupling.

#### Guide D — Limiting case and budget reasoning

- **Variables and ranges:** ridge production fixed or zero; subduction consumption fixed or zero; duration 1–100 Myr; Earth radius assumed constant.
- **Constraint or relationship:** reason about the sign of the global surface-area imbalance without inventing an expanding Earth.
- **Contexts:** hypothetical shutdown of subduction; supercontinent interval; model debugging.
- **Answer formats:** conceptual multiple choice; short prediction; equation-based budget statement.
- **Worked instantiation:** If spreading continued while subduction ceased, a constant-radius long-term crustal budget would be violated; another sink or changed rate would be required.

### Design guidance by purpose

| Purpose | Cognitive level | How to design the task |
|---|---|---|
| Homework | Apply/analyze | Combine a process sequence with one unit-conversion calculation. |
| Group discussion | Evaluate | Compare force explanations for two plate settings and list missing evidence. |
| Quiz | Understand/apply | Ask one age-trend prediction and one crustal-budget item. |
| Exam | Analyze | Integrate cooling trends, area production, and a model limitation. |
| Project/activity | Create | Build a ridge-to-trench transect annotated with material and energy transfers. |

## Objective 1.4a: Use magnetic-stripe and age-distance evidence to test seafloor spreading and calculate spreading rates.

### Target understanding

Mastery means explaining thermoremanent recording, recognizing the joint predictions of stripe symmetry and outward age increase, calculating half-rates and full rates, and qualifying altered or asymmetric records.

### Question guides

#### Guide A — Forward half-rate calculation

- **Variables and ranges:** age 1–180 Ma; perpendicular distance 10–5,000 km; realistic half-rate 5–100 mm/yr.
- **Constraint or relationship:** $v_{1/2}=d/t$ with $1$ km/Myr = $1$ mm/yr; distance is from ridge to one flank.
- **Contexts:** magnetometer profile; dated reversal; ocean-drilling core.
- **Answer formats:** multiple choice; short answer with unit; worked solution showing conversion.
- **Worked instantiation:** A 10 Ma stripe 300 km from the ridge gives 30 mm/yr as the half-rate.

#### Guide B — Inverse age or distance

- **Variables and ranges:** half-rate 5–100 mm/yr; age 1–180 Ma; distance 10–5,000 km.
- **Constraint or relationship:** rearrange $v=d/t$; state whether supplied rate is half or full before solving.
- **Contexts:** planning a core site; locating an expected reversal; checking a map scale.
- **Answer formats:** short value; worked solution; graphical placement.
- **Worked instantiation:** At 25 mm/yr half-rate, 40 Ma crust should lie about 1,000 km from the ridge if the mean rate stayed constant.

#### Guide C — Asymmetric two-flank comparison

- **Variables and ranges:** same reversal age 2–100 Ma; west/east distances differing by 0–20%; half-rates 5–100 mm/yr.
- **Constraint or relationship:** calculate each half-rate and add them for full opening; compare percentage difference.
- **Contexts:** ridge near a plume; migrating ridge axis; imperfectly symmetric spreading.
- **Answer formats:** table; calculation; claim-evidence response.
- **Worked instantiation:** At 8 Ma, 160 km and 184 km yield 20 and 23 mm/yr; full rate = 43 mm/yr.

#### Guide D — Evidence and error analysis

- **Variables and ranges:** sequence length 3–10 bands; normal/reversed polarity; one disturbance such as heating, fault offset, sediment gap, or measurement contamination.
- **Constraint or relationship:** decide whether the pattern supports spreading and identify what additional check is needed.
- **Contexts:** towed magnetometer profile; core magnetization; student stripe sketch.
- **Answer formats:** multiple choice; annotate anomaly; written evaluation.
- **Worked instantiation:** Matching order on both flanks with one locally displaced band supports spreading plus later fault offset; it does not require rejecting the whole model.

### Design guidance by purpose

| Purpose | Cognitive level | How to design the task |
|---|---|---|
| Homework | Apply | Mix forward, inverse, and asymmetric calculations with units. |
| Group discussion | Analyze | Give partial magnetic and age evidence and ask which claim is justified. |
| Quiz | Apply | One half-rate calculation and one symmetry interpretation. |
| Exam | Analyze/evaluate | Include two flanks, uncertainty, and an overprint or fault complication. |
| Project/activity | Create | Construct a scaled synthetic magnetic profile from a reversal sequence and chosen rates. |

## Objective 1.5a: Sequence a Wilson cycle and evaluate plate reconstructions with appropriate limits.

### Target understanding

Mastery means connecting rifting, ocean growth, subduction, closure, and collision to their boundary processes and rock record. Learners should use multiple reconstruction constraints and state that the Wilson cycle is idealized, oceanic records are recycled, and paleomagnetism alone often leaves longitude uncertain.

### Question guides

#### Guide A — Stage identification

- **Variables and ranges:** six idealized stages; evidence bundles of three to five features; basin width from 0 to 8,000 km.
- **Constraint or relationship:** infer the best stage from active boundary and inherited-record evidence; accept qualified alternatives when evidence overlaps.
- **Contexts:** continental rift; narrow sea; mature passive margin; closing basin; suture zone.
- **Answer formats:** matching; short justification; ordered sequence.
- **Worked instantiation:** Passive margins plus a wide ocean and active ridge most strongly indicate a mature-ocean stage.

#### Guide B — Reconstruction evidence synthesis

- **Variables and ranges:** two to five evidence types; ages 50–2,500 Ma; agreement or conflict between constraints.
- **Constraint or relationship:** require at least two independent constraints and one limitation; distinguish latitude/rotation from longitude.
- **Contexts:** assembling a vanished continent; tracing terrane motion; testing a proposed suture.
- **Answer formats:** claim-evidence-reasoning response; evidence matrix; confidence ranking.
- **Worked instantiation:** Complementary margins plus matching dated mountain belts and compatible paleolatitudes strengthen a former-connection claim; relative longitude may remain less certain.

#### Guide C — Compare real basin with ideal cycle

- **Variables and ranges:** missing, repeated, or overlapping stages; asymmetric margins; subduction initiation at one or both sides.
- **Constraint or relationship:** map observations onto the ideal model and name at least one mismatch without forcing every stage.
- **Contexts:** Atlantic opening; remnant sea; failed rift; complex back-arc basin.
- **Answer formats:** table; annotated timeline; evaluative paragraph.
- **Worked instantiation:** A basin with a spreading ridge and subduction on one margin combines mature-ocean growth with closure processes; it need not occupy one pure stage.

#### Guide D — Error analysis and uncertainty

- **Variables and ranges:** one overclaim: exact longitude from one paleomagnetic pole, modern coastline as exact rift edge, all cycles synchronized, every basin follows six stages.
- **Constraint or relationship:** identify the overclaim, preserve the valid inference, and state additional evidence needed.
- **Contexts:** reconstruction animation; museum exhibit; student report.
- **Answer formats:** correction; uncertainty statement; revised caption.
- **Worked instantiation:** A paleomagnetic direction may constrain latitude and rotation, but not a unique longitude; geological correlations are also needed.

### Design guidance by purpose

| Purpose | Cognitive level | How to design the task |
|---|---|---|
| Homework | Apply/analyze | Sequence evidence cards and justify two transitions. |
| Group discussion | Evaluate | Compare two competing reconstructions with an evidence matrix. |
| Quiz | Understand | Identify stages and one diagnostic feature. |
| Exam | Analyze/evaluate | Present conflicting evidence and require a qualified reconstruction claim. |
| Project/activity | Create | Build a basin-history storyboard that distinguishes observation from inference. |

## Rubric Themes for Chapter 1

| Evidence of mastery | What to look for |
|---|---|
| Correct categories | Crust/mantle and lithosphere/asthenosphere are not conflated. |
| Geometry-first reasoning | Relative-motion arrows precede boundary labels and predictions. |
| Coupled-system explanation | Creation, cooling, density, subduction, and force balance are linked. |
| Quantitative care | Half/full rate, unit conversion, and assumptions are explicit. |
| Evidence-based inference | Multiple observations support a claim; alternatives are considered. |
| Appropriate uncertainty | Idealized boundary and Wilson-cycle models are applied without overclaiming. |
| Communication | Diagrams, terms, units, and causal language are clear and consistent. |
