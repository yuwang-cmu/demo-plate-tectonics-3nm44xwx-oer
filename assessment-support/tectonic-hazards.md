# Assessment Design Guide: Tectonic Hazards—Processes, Warning, and Risk

## Purpose and Alignment

Use assessment to reveal causal reasoning, not recall of famous disasters. Every task should identify its evidence, require a defensible inference, and preserve uncertainty. Parameters below let an instructor vary setting, data representation, audience, or decision without turning this guide into a question bank.

## Objective 2.1a–b: Fault Style and Elastic Rebound

Target cognition: apply and explain.

- **Motion-to-fault task:** provide two velocity arrows with adjustable perpendicular and parallel components. Ask learners to identify extension, shortening, shear, or oblique deformation; propose a plausible fault style; and state what remains uncertain without subsurface geometry.
- **Sequence task:** provide loading, locking, strain, rupture, wave radiation, and renewed loading in scrambled order. Require a causal sequence and a sentence explaining why it is not a regular clock.
- **Scale task:** contrast a multi-year geodetic trend with a seconds-long seismogram. Ask which records loading, which records rupture, and how they are connected.

Evidence of mastery: learners use relative motion before fault names, distinguish hypocenter from epicenter, and reject deterministic recurrence.

Common diagnostic error: treating the earthquake as the cause of plate motion rather than a consequence of accumulated deformation.

## Objective 2.2a–b: Seismic Observations and Uneven Shaking

Target cognition: interpret and analyze.

- **Arrival-order task:** give qualitative P- and S-wave arrival sequences at three stations. Ask which station is closest and why multiple stations are needed for location.
- **Magnitude–intensity task:** hold magnitude constant while varying distance, sediment, and building design. Ask learners to rank expected intensity only when evidence supports it and name additional information needed.
- **Logarithmic-scale task:** choose two whole-number magnitudes separated by one or two units. Require use of the approximate 32-fold energy factor and a warning that energy ratio does not equal damage ratio.
- **Claim critique:** present “both towns experienced the same earthquake, so damage should be equal.” Require at least three physical or structural reasons the inference fails.

Evidence of mastery: one source magnitude, place-varying intensity; correct causal factors; no misuse of “Richter scale” as a generic local-damage label.

## Objective 2.3a–b: Volcano Hazards and Monitoring

Target cognition: compare and evaluate.

- **Hazard-pathway matrix:** vary lava viscosity, gas retention, slope, snow or water availability, and wind. Ask which hazards become more plausible and which pathways carry them.
- **Multi-signal interpretation:** provide qualitative time series for earthquake rate, deformation, gas output, and temperature. Require an interpretation that cites converging or conflicting signals and avoids a certain eruption time.
- **Decision threshold task:** assign roles such as observatory, school, aviation operator, or valley community. Ask what evidence would justify an action with different costs of false alarms and missed events.
- **Cascade map:** begin with eruption, flank collapse, or ash fall and require two downstream infrastructure or health consequences plus an intervention point.

Evidence of mastery: learners distinguish lava, tephra, pyroclastic density currents, lahars, and gases, and combine independent observations.

## Objective 2.4a–b: Tsunami Generation and Shoaling

Target cognition: model and apply.

- **Source diagnosis:** vary earthquake location, depth, fault motion, vertical seafloor displacement, landslide, or eruption. Ask whether rapid large-volume displacement is plausible and what observation would test it.
- **Speed calculation:** provide water depth and $c=\sqrt{gd}$. Require units, an approximate speed, and a statement that coastal bathymetry changes travel and inundation.
- **Concept sketch:** ask learners to label deep-ocean wavelength and height, nearshore speed and wavelength, run-up, and multiple-wave behavior.
- **Safety-message critique:** compare a message that waits for shoreline withdrawal with one based on strong or long shaking and official guidance. Ask which assumptions make the first unsafe.

Evidence of mastery: not every offshore earthquake is tsunamigenic; deep-ocean low height does not imply low coastal hazard; the first wave need not be the largest.

## Objective 2.5a–b: Warning and Risk Reduction

Target cognition: evaluate and design.

- **Four-term classification:** provide statements about monitoring, probabilistic forecasting, exact prediction, and early warning. Require classification plus correction of one misleading statement.
- **Latency budget:** provide qualitative or numerical detection, processing, communication, and action times. Ask whether useful warning remains for near and distant users.
- **Risk-chain analysis:** vary hazard, exposure, structural vulnerability, communication access, and ability to evacuate. Ask which interventions change which part of the chain.
- **Equity audit:** provide a technically accurate alert that excludes a language, sensory, mobility, or connectivity group. Ask learners to redesign the system and explain why access is part of physical risk reduction.
- **Portfolio task:** allocate a constrained budget among monitoring, retrofit, land-use planning, education, and redundant communication. Require a defense against at least two hazard scenarios.

Evidence of mastery: early warning follows event onset, forecasts are probabilistic, and risk reduction extends beyond sensors.

## Balanced Assessment Blueprint

| Objective group | Suggested weight | Minimum evidence |
|---|---:|---|
| 2.1 Fault process | 20% | Motion interpretation plus causal explanation |
| 2.2 Seismic observation | 20% | Wave or magnitude/intensity reasoning |
| 2.3 Volcanic systems | 20% | Multi-hazard or multi-signal analysis |
| 2.4 Tsunami systems | 20% | Generation plus propagation or safety reasoning |
| 2.5 Warning and risk | 20% | System-chain evaluation with a feasible action |

Weights are a design recommendation, not empirical data. Adjust them to instructional time while retaining evidence from every objective group.

## Question Guide Crosswalk

- Objectives 2.1a–b: motion-to-fault, causal sequence, and timescale comparison.
- Objectives 2.2a–b: wave arrivals, magnitude–intensity comparison, and claim critique.
- Objectives 2.3a–b: hazard pathways, multiple monitoring streams, and decision thresholds.
- Objectives 2.4a–b: source diagnosis, wave-speed modeling, and safety-message critique.
- Objectives 2.5a–b: terminology classification, latency budgets, risk chains, and equity audits.

## Rubric Framework

| Criterion | Beginning | Developing | Secure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Physical mechanism | Names a hazard without a cause | Gives a partial process chain | Links motion, mechanism, observation, and limit |
| Evidence use | Makes an unsupported claim | Cites one relevant observation | Combines appropriate evidence and alternatives |
| Uncertainty | Claims certainty or gives no decision | Notes uncertainty without using it | States uncertainty and ties action to thresholds |
| Risk reasoning | Treats hazard as inevitable disaster | Mentions exposure or vulnerability | Connects hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and feasible action |
| Communication | Uses ambiguous terms or missing units | Mostly clear with minor omissions | Uses defined terms, units, accessible explanation, and a defensible conclusion |

## Accessibility and Fairness Checks

- Describe every map, diagram, or graph in text and do not require color alone.
- Define specialized emergency-management terms before using them in scoring criteria.
- Avoid testing prior familiarity with a named disaster unless all necessary context is supplied.
- Offer equivalent text data when interpreting a visual record.
- Score the reasoning chain separately from arithmetic slips when calculation is not the main objective.
- Evaluate whether a proposed protective action is realistically available to the population described.
