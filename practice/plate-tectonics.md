# Chapter 1 Practice: Plate Tectonics

These 15 questions are instantiated from the assessment guide and grouped across all five objectives. Open each Answer tab after making your own attempt; calculations show the setup and units.

## Mechanical Layers and Moving Plates

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 1
**(Obj 1.1a · multiple choice)** A boundary at 95 km depth separates rock that remains rigid over long periods above from rock that deforms viscously below. Which label is best supported?

A. Crust–mantle boundary  
B. Lithosphere–asthenosphere boundary  
C. Oceanic–continental boundary  
D. Inner-core–outer-core boundary
:::
::: tab Answer
**B. Lithosphere–asthenosphere boundary.** The evidence describes a change in mechanical behavior. The crust–mantle boundary is a compositional distinction and is not defined by long-term rigidity alone.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 2
**(Obj 1.1a · short answer)** Correct this claim: “The asthenosphere is a global layer of liquid magma on which plates float.”
:::
::: tab Answer
The asthenosphere is **mostly solid upper-mantle rock**. At its temperature and pressure, it is mechanically weaker than the lithosphere and can deform viscously over geologic time. Local melting occurs in some settings, but the layer is not a global magma ocean.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 3
**(Obj 1.1a · comparison)** Oceanic lithosphere at location A is 5 Ma old; at location B it is 120 Ma old. Assuming ordinary ridge-to-basin cooling and no unusual reheating, rank A and B by lithospheric thickness and explain.
:::
::: tab Answer
**B is thicker than A.** As oceanic lithosphere moves away from a ridge, conductive cooling allows more upper mantle to become part of the cool, mechanically rigid lithosphere. This thickens the mantle-lithosphere portion even though the basaltic crust does not thicken in the same way.
:::

## Boundary Kinematics and Surface Expression

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 4
**(Obj 1.2a · vector interpretation)** Two plates have relative velocity components of 42 mm/yr toward their boundary and 9 mm/yr parallel to it. Classify the dominant motion and identify the secondary component.
:::
::: tab Answer
The dominant motion is **convergence** because the perpendicular component is 42 mm/yr toward the boundary. The 9 mm/yr parallel component adds lateral shear, so the setting is **obliquely convergent**, not perfectly head-on.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 5
**(Obj 1.2a · evidence inference)** A region has a deep ocean trench, an arc of volcanoes, and earthquakes whose foci become deeper away from the trench. What boundary process best explains the pattern?
:::
::: tab Answer
The pattern supports **subduction**. The trench marks bending of oceanic lithosphere, the dipping earthquake zone traces the descending slab, and water released from the slab promotes melting above it, feeding the volcanic arc.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 6
**(Obj 1.2a · error analysis)** A student draws arrows pointing apart across an ocean ridge but labels earthquakes down to 600 km beneath the ridge. Identify the mismatch and correct it.
:::
::: tab Answer
Divergent ridges mainly produce **shallow earthquakes** as brittle lithosphere fractures near the surface. A zone of earthquakes extending hundreds of kilometers deep is characteristic of a **descending slab at a convergent subduction zone**, not an ordinary ridge.
:::
::::

## Creation, Cooling, and Recycling

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 7
**(Obj 1.3a · sequencing)** Put these events in a defensible order for a parcel of oceanic lithosphere: subduction, basalt solidifies at a ridge, density increases, conductive cooling, outward motion.
:::
::: tab Answer
1. Basalt solidifies at a ridge.  
2. The plate carries it outward.  
3. Conductive cooling proceeds.  
4. The lithosphere thickens and its average density increases.  
5. Mature oceanic lithosphere may enter a subduction zone.

The sequence connects creation to recycling; not every parcel must be subducted on the same schedule.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 8
**(Obj 1.3a · calculation)** A 2,400-km-long ridge segment has a full spreading rate of 75 mm/yr. Estimate the total area of new seafloor produced each year across both flanks.
:::
::: tab Answer
Convert the widening rate:

$$75\ \text{mm/yr}=7.5\times10^{-5}\ \text{km/yr}.$$

Then multiply by ridge length:

$$A=(2400\ \text{km})(7.5\times10^{-5}\ \text{km/yr})=0.180\ \text{km}^2/\text{yr}.$$

The estimate assumes the stated length and full rate remain constant.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 9
**(Obj 1.3a · evaluation)** Plate X is attached to a long, cold slab descending into the mantle. Plate Y has no major subduction zone along its edges. Is “slab pull explains everything” an adequate comparison? Explain.
:::
::: tab Answer
No. A long cold slab makes **strong slab pull plausible for Plate X**, but plate motion is a coupled force balance involving boundary resistance, mantle flow, gravitational sliding, and geometry. Plate Y's motion shows that a plate can move without being directly attached to a major sinking slab; its surrounding system must be evaluated separately.
:::

## Magnetic Stripes and Spreading Rates

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 10
**(Obj 1.4a · forward calculation)** A magnetic reversal dated at 12 Ma lies 360 km from a ridge axis on one flank. Calculate the mean half-spreading rate in mm/yr.
:::
::: tab Answer
$$v_{1/2}=\frac{d}{t}=\frac{360\ \text{km}}{12\ \text{Myr}}=30\ \text{km/Myr}.$$

Because $1\ \text{km/Myr}=1\ \text{mm/yr}$,

$$v_{1/2}=30\ \text{mm/yr}.$$

This is a half-rate because the distance runs from the ridge to one flank.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 11
**(Obj 1.4a · inverse calculation)** A ridge has maintained a mean half-spreading rate of 25 mm/yr for 40 Myr. How far from the ridge should 40 Ma crust lie, ignoring later deformation?
:::
::: tab Answer
Use $d=vt$. Since $25\ \text{mm/yr}=25\ \text{km/Myr}$,

$$d=(25\ \text{km/Myr})(40\ \text{Myr})=1000\ \text{km}.$$

The predicted location is **1,000 km from the ridge** on that flank.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 12
**(Obj 1.4a · asymmetric comparison)** The same 20 Ma reversal is 500 km west of a ridge and 560 km east. Find both half-rates and the full opening rate.
:::
::: tab Answer
West:

$$v_W=500/20=25\ \text{km/Myr}=25\ \text{mm/yr}.$$

East:

$$v_E=560/20=28\ \text{km/Myr}=28\ \text{mm/yr}.$$

Full rate:

$$v_{full}=v_W+v_E=53\ \text{mm/yr}.$$

The unequal half-rates indicate modest asymmetry.
:::

## Wilson Cycles and Reconstruction

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 13
**(Obj 1.5a · stage identification)** A region contains a wide ocean with an active central ridge and passive continental margins. Which idealized Wilson-cycle stage fits best, and what process dominates?
:::
::: tab Answer
The best fit is the **mature-ocean stage**. Long-lived seafloor spreading at the ridge widens the basin while passive margins lie away from an active plate boundary. A real ocean may also have subduction elsewhere, so the label is an idealized dominant state.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 14
**(Obj 1.5a · evidence synthesis)** Two continental margins have complementary geometry. Name two independent evidence types that could strengthen a claim that they were formerly joined, and state one limitation.
:::
::: tab Answer
Strengthening evidence could include matching dated rock belts or mountain structures, compatible fossil or sedimentary provinces, and paleomagnetic directions indicating consistent paleolatitudes and rotations. A limitation is that modern coastlines are modified and are not exact original rift edges; paleomagnetism also may not determine unique longitude.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 15
**(Obj 1.5a · model critique)** A basin has an active spreading ridge but also a subduction zone on one margin. A classmate says it cannot be placed in a Wilson-cycle model because opening and closing happen at the same time. Evaluate the claim.
:::
::: tab Answer
The claim is too rigid. The Wilson cycle is an **idealized organizing model**, not a rule that every basin occupies one pure stage. The ridge adds oceanic lithosphere while the subduction zone removes it. Whether the basin grows or shrinks depends on the relative rates and geometry. The basin can therefore combine mature-ocean and closing processes.
:::
