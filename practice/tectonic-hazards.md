# Practice: Tectonic Hazards—Processes, Warning, and Risk

:::info
Each item pairs a question with a worked answer. Explain the causal link, not only the vocabulary. Numerical answers retain units and state the model limit.

## Fault Loading and Rupture

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 1
**(Objective 2.1a)** Two crustal blocks move directly apart. Which first-order fault style is most plausible, and what deformation does it accommodate?
:::
::: tab Answer
A **normal fault** is most plausible because it accommodates extension. The answer is first-order: local faults can be oblique or distributed, so field or subsurface geometry is still needed.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 2
**(Objective 2.1b)** Put these in causal order: seismic waves radiate; a fault locks; plate motion loads the region; rupture propagates; elastic strain accumulates.
:::
::: tab Answer
Plate motion loads the region → the fault remains locked → elastic strain accumulates → rupture begins and propagates → seismic waves radiate. Loading can continue afterward, but the intervals and rupture sizes are not regular.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 3
**(Objective 2.1b)** A news report says the epicenter is the underground point where rupture started. Correct the statement.
:::
::: tab Answer
The underground initial rupture point is the **hypocenter** or focus. The **epicenter** is the point at Earth’s surface directly above the hypocenter.
:::
::::

## Seismic Observations

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 4
**(Objective 2.2a)** Why can a P-wave detection sometimes contribute to an alert before stronger shaking reaches a more distant location?
:::
::: tab Answer
P waves travel faster through rock than S waves. A nearby station can detect the P wave, estimate the event, and communicate electronically while slower, often more damaging motion is still traveling. Processing, communication, and response time reduce the usable interval, and a nearby user may receive no useful warning.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 5
**(Objective 2.2b)** An earthquake has magnitude 6.8. Town A on soft sediment shakes more strongly than Town B on bedrock. Is this a contradiction?
:::
::: tab Answer
No. Magnitude describes the source event with one value. Intensity or ground motion varies by location. Soft sediment can amplify some shaking, while distance, wave path, topography, and structures also affect local observations.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 6
**(Objective 2.2b)** Using about 32 times more released energy per magnitude unit, compare magnitude 6 and magnitude 4 events.
:::
::: tab Answer
The difference is two units, so the approximate ratio is $32^2=1024$. The magnitude 6 source releases roughly one thousand times as much energy. This does not mean every location suffers one thousand times the damage.
:::
::::

## Volcano Processes and Monitoring

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 7
**(Objective 2.3a)** Distinguish magma, lava, and tephra.
:::
::: tab Answer
**Magma** is molten or partly molten material below the surface. **Lava** is magma that emerges and flows at the surface. **Tephra** is fragmented solid material ejected into the air.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 8
**(Objective 2.3a)** Why is “volcanic hazard” too broad for an evacuation plan? Name three pathways that demand different decisions.
:::
::: tab Answer
Different hazards move differently. A lava flow follows topography and may allow route planning; airborne ash follows plume height and wind and affects roofs, aviation, water, and breathing; a lahar moves rapidly along valleys and can occur during or after eruption. Pyroclastic density currents and gases add other distinct pathways.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 9
**(Objective 2.3b)** Seismicity increases, but deformation and gas observations remain near baseline. What is a defensible interpretation?
:::
::: tab Answer
The seismic change deserves investigation, but one stream does not prove magma ascent or an imminent eruption. Check event location and type, instrument performance, and additional signals; compare with the volcano’s baseline; state alternative explanations and uncertainty.
:::
::::

## Tsunami Systems

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 10
**(Objective 2.4a)** Why does an offshore earthquake not automatically generate a tsunami?
:::
::: tab Answer
A tsunami requires rapid displacement of a large water volume. Source depth, fault geometry, slip, and especially vertical seafloor displacement matter. Some offshore ruptures move mainly horizontally or displace too little water.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 11
**(Objective 2.4b)** Estimate tsunami speed for $d=5000\ \text{m}$ using $c=\sqrt{gd}$ and $g\approx10\ \text{m/s}^2$.
:::
::: tab Answer
$c=\sqrt{10\times5000}=\sqrt{50000}\approx224\ \text{m/s}$. Multiplying by 3.6 gives about $806\ \text{km/h}$. This approximate deep-ocean speed does not determine local run-up or account for detailed bathymetry.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 12
**(Objective 2.4b)** A person says, “I will evacuate only if the sea withdraws.” Identify two problems.
:::
::: tab Answer
Withdrawal occurs only if a trough arrives first, so it is not guaranteed. Waiting also wastes time after strong or long coastal shaking or an official warning. Safer action is prompt movement using local evacuation guidance, followed by waiting for an official all-clear because several waves may arrive.
:::
::::

## Warning and Risk Reduction

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 13
**(Objective 2.5a)** Classify each statement: “a 30% chance in the next decade,” “rupture detected; take cover,” and “gas output is rising.”
:::
::: tab Answer
“30% chance in the next decade” is a **forecast**. “Rupture detected; take cover” is **early warning** because the event has begun. “Gas output is rising” is a **monitoring observation** that requires interpretation.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 14
**(Objective 2.5a)** Why is earthquake early warning not earthquake prediction?
:::
::: tab Answer
Early warning detects and analyzes an earthquake **after rupture begins**, then tries to deliver information before damaging shaking reaches some users. Prediction would specify a future event’s time, place, and size before onset.
:::
::::

:::: tabs
::: tab Q 15
**(Objective 2.5b)** A coastal town has accurate sensors but fragile buildings, one siren, no accessible messages, and an evacuation route that crosses a low bridge. Name four improvements and the part of risk each addresses.
:::
::: tab Answer
Retrofit buildings to reduce structural **vulnerability**; add redundant, accessible, multilingual communication to strengthen **warning reach**; redesign or add evacuation routes to improve **feasible action** and reduce exposure during inundation; conduct inclusive drills so people can interpret and act on messages. Sensors help characterize hazard, but the other links determine whether information becomes protection.
:::
