# Course Concept Map: Plate Tectonics and Tectonic Hazards

## Overall Summary

Plate tectonics explains large-scale patterns at Earth's surface by treating the lithosphere as moving, internally coherent plates above a weaker asthenosphere. The central reasoning chain runs from material behavior to relative motion, then from relative motion to boundary processes, observable evidence, and long-term reconstruction.

The course is organized as a model-to-decision sequence. Chapter 1 defines what moves, predicts what each plate interaction should produce, and tests those predictions with geophysical evidence. Chapter 2 carries the same causal model into earthquakes, volcanoes, tsunamis, monitoring, warning, and risk reduction. A boundary process creates a hazard; exposure and vulnerability help determine whether that hazard becomes a disaster.

## Major Concepts and Dependencies

- Mechanical structure of the outer Earth
  - Crust and mantle are compositional categories.
  - Lithosphere and asthenosphere are mechanical categories.
  - A rigid lithosphere over a weaker asthenosphere enables plate-like motion.
- Relative plate motion
  - Motion apart produces divergent boundaries.
  - Motion together produces convergence, subduction, or continental collision.
  - Lateral motion produces transform boundaries.
- Oceanic-lithosphere cycle
  - Divergence creates oceanic crust at ridges.
  - Cooling thickens and increases the density of oceanic lithosphere.
  - Mature oceanic lithosphere can sink at subduction zones.
- Evidence and inference
  - Symmetric magnetic anomalies test the spreading model.
  - Rock age and distance constrain spreading rates.
  - Earthquake depth and topography help locate active boundaries.
- Basin-scale synthesis
  - Rifting can develop into a young and then mature ocean.
  - Subduction can reverse basin growth and lead to collision.
  - Sutures, magnetic records, and plate geometry support reconstructions.
- Hazard-generating processes
  - Locked faults accumulate elastic strain and sometimes rupture.
  - Magma movement and eruption create several distinct volcanic hazards.
  - Rapid water displacement can launch a long-wavelength tsunami.
- Observation and warning
  - Seismic waves reveal rupture location and size after rupture begins.
  - Volcano monitoring compares seismicity, deformation, gases, and thermal observations.
  - Tsunami warning combines earthquake information, water-level observations, models, and evacuation.
- Risk as a coupled system
  - Hazard describes a potentially damaging process or event.
  - Exposure and vulnerability shape consequences.
  - Engineering, land-use choices, education, and practiced response can reduce risk.

## Teaching Order and Reinforcement Links

1. Define the moving layer before discussing motion.
2. Classify motion geometrically before predicting landforms or hazards.
3. Link ridge creation to subduction so Earth's nearly constant surface area is explicit.
4. Test predictions with independent observations rather than presenting the theory as a list of facts.
5. Reuse the boundary model in the Wilson cycle to explain basin evolution.
6. Carry boundary kinematics into Chapter 2: divergence links to normal faulting, convergence to thrust faulting and many tsunamigenic earthquakes, and transform motion to strike-slip rupture.
7. Separate event size from local effects, and hazard from risk, before evaluating warnings or mitigation.

The same ideas reinforce one another: cooling changes density, density helps explain subduction, subduction helps close the global crustal budget, and that budget makes the Wilson cycle physically coherent.

Chapter 2 reinforces Chapter 1 by turning predictions into observations and decisions. Plate motion loads faults; rupture generates waves; magma movement changes monitoring signals; and seafloor displacement launches waves whose coastal effects depend on bathymetry. Monitoring can shorten uncertainty after a process begins, but it does not remove exposure or vulnerability.

## Section Objectives

### 1.1 Mechanical Layers and Moving Plates

- Distinguish compositional layers from mechanical layers.
- Explain why the lithosphere can behave as plates over geologic time.

### 1.2 Boundary Kinematics and Surface Expression

- Classify a boundary from relative-motion information.
- Predict characteristic landforms, volcanism, and earthquake patterns while recognizing exceptions.

### 1.3 Creation, Cooling, and Recycling of Oceanic Lithosphere

- Trace oceanic lithosphere from a spreading ridge to a subduction zone.
- Compare slab pull, gravitational sliding from ridges, and mantle-flow coupling without reducing motion to one universal cause.

### 1.4 Reading the Magnetic and Age Record

- Explain why marine magnetic stripes are mirror images across many ridges.
- Calculate half-spreading and full-spreading rates from distance and age.

### 1.5 Ocean-Basin Life Cycles and Model Limits

- Sequence the main stages of a Wilson cycle.
- Use multiple evidence types to make and qualify a plate reconstruction.

### 2.1 Fault Loading and Earthquake Rupture

- Relate plate-boundary motion to normal, reverse, strike-slip, and oblique faulting.
- Explain elastic rebound without implying a regularly timed cycle.

### 2.2 Seismic Observations and Uneven Shaking

- Distinguish hypocenter from epicenter and magnitude from intensity.
- Explain why wave type, distance, local geology, and structures affect consequences.

### 2.3 Volcano Processes and Cascading Hazards

- Connect tectonic setting and magma behavior to eruption products and hazards.
- Interpret multiple monitoring signals without treating any one signal as a certain forecast.

### 2.4 Tsunami Generation, Propagation, and Inundation

- Identify processes that displace enough water to generate a tsunami.
- Explain deep-ocean propagation, shoaling, run-up, and the danger of multiple waves.

### 2.5 Warning, Preparedness, and Risk Reduction

- Distinguish prediction, forecasting, early warning, and monitoring.
- Evaluate how exposure, vulnerability, communication, and protective action shape risk.
