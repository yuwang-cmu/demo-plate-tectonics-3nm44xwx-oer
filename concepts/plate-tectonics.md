# Chapter 1 Concept Map: Plate Tectonics

## Source and Scope

This chapter adapts five English Wikipedia articles whose exact revisions are recorded in `inputs/SOURCE_CORPUS.json`. It covers mechanical layering, boundary kinematics, ocean-floor production and recycling, paleomagnetic evidence, spreading-rate reasoning, and the Wilson cycle. This file uses simple Markdown only.

## Chapter Summary

Plate tectonics is a kinematic and dynamic model of Earth's rigid outer shell. The lithosphere includes crust plus the uppermost mantle and is separated from the asthenosphere by mechanical behavior, not by a change from one chemical layer to another. Plates may carry oceanic crust, continental crust, or both.

Relative motion at plate edges organizes most of the chapter. Divergence creates space for new oceanic crust, convergence consumes oceanic lithosphere or thickens continental crust, and transform motion accommodates lateral displacement. The model becomes persuasive when these predictions are compared with seafloor ages, magnetic polarity bands, earthquake locations and depths, ridge and trench topography, and the distribution of volcanic arcs.

At a longer timescale, the Wilson cycle links rifting, ocean growth, subduction, closure, and collision. It is a useful organizing model, not a claim that every basin follows a perfectly timed six-stage script.

## Core Dependency Chain

- Heat escapes from Earth's interior.
  - This enables: thermal contrasts and mantle deformation over long timescales.
- The cool outer shell behaves rigidly relative to the hotter asthenosphere.
  - This enables: coherent lithospheric plates.
- Plates move relative to one another.
  - This enables: divergent, convergent, and transform boundaries.
- Boundaries create, deform, or recycle lithosphere.
  - This enables: ridges, trenches, arcs, mountain belts, faults, and earthquakes.
- Rocks preserve age, orientation, and magnetic information.
  - This enables: tests of spreading and reconstruction of past motion.
- Boundary configurations change through time.
  - This enables: opening and closing ocean basins in a Wilson-cycle framework.

## Section-Level Concept Map and Objectives

### 1.1 Mechanical Layers and Moving Plates

**Learning objectives:**

- Distinguish crust/mantle composition from lithosphere/asthenosphere mechanics.
- Explain how rigid plates can move above material that remains solid but deforms over geologic time.

**Concept flow:**

- Chemical layering: crust over mantle.
- Mechanical layering: rigid lithosphere over weaker asthenosphere.
  - Lithosphere = crust + lithospheric mantle.
  - Asthenosphere = hotter upper mantle capable of viscous deformation.
- Horizontal subdivision of lithosphere.
  - Plates may include both oceanic and continental regions.

### 1.2 Boundary Kinematics and Surface Expression

**Learning objectives:**

- Classify a boundary from the direction of relative motion.
- Predict first-order geological expressions and explain why broad or oblique boundaries can be mixed.

**Concept flow:**

- Relative motion apart.
  - Divergence, extension, ridges or rifts, shallow earthquakes, volcanism.
- Relative motion together.
  - Subduction if sufficiently dense oceanic lithosphere sinks.
  - Collision and crustal thickening when buoyant continents meet.
- Relative motion parallel to boundary.
  - Transform faulting and shallow earthquakes; little net crust creation or destruction.

### 1.3 Creation, Cooling, and Recycling of Oceanic Lithosphere

**Learning objectives:**

- Trace changes in oceanic lithosphere with increasing age and distance from a ridge.
- Compare principal contributions to plate motion without treating one mechanism as universal.

**Concept flow:**

- Ridge volcanism creates basaltic oceanic crust.
- Conductive cooling away from ridge.
  - Lithospheric mantle thickens.
  - Density increases and seafloor subsides.
- At subduction zones, old oceanic lithosphere returns to the mantle.
- Motion reflects a coupled system.
  - Slab pull is widely regarded as a strong driver where slabs sink.
  - Gravitational sliding from elevated ridges contributes.
  - Mantle flow and plate geometry interact.

### 1.4 Reading the Magnetic and Age Record

**Learning objectives:**

- Explain thermoremanent magnetic recording and mirror-image marine anomalies.
- Compute and interpret spreading rates from an age-distance pair.

**Concept flow:**

- Basalt cools below magnetic-mineral blocking temperatures.
  - Some minerals preserve the field direction at cooling.
- Earth's field reverses irregularly.
  - Successive crustal bands preserve normal and reversed polarity.
- Crust forms at the ridge and moves outward on both sides.
  - Matching stripe sequence and symmetry test spreading.
  - Distance divided by age gives a half-spreading rate for one flank.
  - Full basin-opening rate is the sum of the two half-rates.

### 1.5 Ocean-Basin Life Cycles and Model Limits

**Learning objectives:**

- Sequence the idealized stages from continental rifting to collision.
- Evaluate a reconstruction using several constraints and identify what remains uncertain.

**Concept flow:**

- Extension localizes into a continental rift.
- Continued spreading creates a young sea and then a mature ocean.
- Subduction begins and oceanic lithosphere is consumed.
- Basin narrows; continents collide; a suture and mountain belt remain.
- Later extension may exploit a zone of inherited weakness.
- Reconstructions combine geometric fit, magnetic stripes, paleomagnetic latitude, fossils, rock belts, and structures.
  - Evidence coverage decreases backward in time.
  - Paleomagnetism constrains latitude and rotation better than longitude.
  - Real basin histories may be asymmetric, interrupted, or out of sequence.

## Common Student Bottlenecks

| Bottleneck | Conceptual repair |
|---|---|
| Treating crust and lithosphere as synonyms | Draw the chemical and mechanical classifications side by side; the lithosphere includes upper mantle. |
| Imagining the asthenosphere as a global magma ocean | Emphasize solid-state viscous deformation over long timescales. |
| Memorizing boundary landforms without relative motion | Start every case with two velocity arrows and classify the geometry first. |
| Saying magma pressure pushes plates apart everywhere | Separate crust production at ridges from the coupled force balance of slabs, gravity, and mantle flow. |
| Doubling or failing to double a spreading rate | Name the measured quantity: ridge-to-one-flank distance gives a half-rate; flank-to-flank separation gives a full rate. |
| Treating magnetic stripes as a compass map of current north | Link polarity bands to rock formation time and later outward transport. |
| Treating the Wilson cycle as a clockwork law | Use it as an idealized sequence and compare each real basin with evidence and exceptions. |
